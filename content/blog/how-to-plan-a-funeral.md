# How to Plan a Funeral: A Practical Step-by-Step Guide

Planning a funeral involves making dozens of important decisions — often within 24 to 72 hours of a death — while simultaneously processing grief, managing family dynamics, and dealing with practical logistics. It is one of the most challenging experiences most people ever face.

This guide walks you through the entire process, step by step, so you know what to expect and what to do at each stage.

## Step 1: Obtain the Death Certificate

Before anything else can proceed, the death must be officially documented. If your loved one died at a hospital or care facility, staff will notify the appropriate authorities. If the death occurred at home, call 911 or the person's hospice provider.

A physician or medical examiner must certify the death and sign the death certificate. You will need multiple certified copies — typically 8 to 12 — for insurance claims, financial institutions, government agencies, and property transfers. Your funeral director will help you obtain these.

## Step 2: Contact a Funeral Home

Contact a funeral home as soon as possible after the death. The funeral home will arrange to transport the body from the place of death to their facility, where it will be cared for until arrangements are finalized.

You are not required to use the funeral home closest to where the death occurred. You have the right to choose any licensed funeral home, including one in another city if the burial will take place elsewhere.

## Step 3: Gather Important Documents

While the funeral home begins its work, gather the following documents. Your funeral director will need many of these:

- Birth certificate
- Social Security card or number
- Military discharge papers (DD-214) if applicable
- Marriage certificate if applicable
- Insurance policies with funeral benefit provisions
- Any pre-arranged funeral plans

## Step 4: Notify Family and Friends

Contact immediate family members first, then close friends. If the deceased had an address book, work through it. Consider whether a social media announcement is appropriate for your family — many families find that a brief post on the deceased's profile or a family member's page helps notify a wider circle quickly.

## Step 5: Make the Arrangements

Meet with the funeral director to make the formal arrangements. This meeting will cover:

- Burial or cremation
- Type of service and location
- Casket or urn selection
- Embalming and preparation of the body
- Obituary preparation
- Death certificate copies
- Transportation
- Cemetery arrangements if applicable

Bring a family member or trusted friend to this meeting if possible. Having a second set of ears helps ensure nothing is missed and provides emotional support.

Request an itemized Statement of Funeral Goods and Services Selected before agreeing to anything. Review it carefully.

## Step 6: Plan the Service

Work with the funeral director, clergy, or a celebrant to plan the service. Consider:

- Who will officiate?
- Who will deliver eulogies?
- What music will be played?
- What readings or scriptures will be included?
- Will there be a photo display or video tribute?
- Will there be an open or closed casket?
- What flowers or decorations are appropriate?

Contact the people you would like to speak at the service as early as possible to give them time to prepare.

## Step 7: Write the Obituary

The obituary announces the death to the community and provides a brief biography of the deceased. Most funeral homes assist with obituary writing and placement in local newspapers and online platforms.

A standard obituary includes full name and age, hometown, date of death, surviving family members, a brief biography, service details, and information about donations in lieu of flowers if the family wishes.

## Step 8: Coordinate Cemetery Arrangements

If burial is planned, contact the cemetery to arrange the plot, opening and closing, and any monument or grave marker. Cemetery arrangements are separate from funeral home arrangements and involve separate costs.

## Step 9: Manage Practical Logistics on the Day

On the day of the service, arrive early. The funeral home staff will guide you through the day, but having a trusted family member or friend assigned to specific tasks — directing guests, managing flowers, coordinating with the clergy — reduces pressure on the immediate family.

## Step 10: After the Service

In the days and weeks following the service, there are additional practical matters to address. These include notifying Social Security, the deceased's employer, financial institutions, and government agencies; canceling subscriptions and accounts; and beginning the probate process if required.

Allow yourself and your family time to grieve before diving into these administrative tasks. Most can wait a week or two.

## A Final Note

No funeral is perfect. Unexpected things happen. What matters is not the perfection of the arrangements but the love behind them. The people who gather to honor your loved one are not there to judge the flowers or critique the music. They are there because they loved the same person you did.

Give yourself grace. You are doing something profoundly difficult with great love.
